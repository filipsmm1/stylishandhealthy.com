Medicube PDRN Pink Peptide Serum Review: Is “Salmon DNA” Skincare Worth the Hype?

A formula-first review of the evidence, limitations, safety, and who should skip it.

Written by Filip - Researcher & Writer at Stylish & Healthy

Filip researches skincare, hair science, and wellness claims for Stylish & Healthy, comparing product marketing with clinical studies, regulatory guidance, and primary research.

Published and last updated: July 4, 2026

This article is not medically reviewed. It is based on peer-reviewed studies, official regulatory sources, and the current product page. Medicube did not sponsor this review.

Stylishandhealthy24 min readEvidence audit

Medicube’s PDRN Pink Peptide Serum is built around a headline that is almost impossible to ignore: salmon DNA for brighter, firmer, more resilient skin. PDRN is not a fake ingredient. It has legitimate pharmacology and a real history in wound care. The problem is that most of that evidence does not study a normal cosmetic serum sitting on intact facial skin.

The finished formula can still be useful. It contains glycerin, niacinamide, sodium hyaluronate, adenosine, five peptides, and several emollient and film-forming ingredients. Those ingredients may support hydration, surface plumping, and gradual improvement in dullness or fine lines. What they cannot do is prove that the Sodium DNA in this bottle recreates the effects seen with injected PDRN or PDRN applied directly to open wounds.

Testing disclosure

Stylish & Healthy did not conduct a hands-on wear test of this serum. This is an ingredient, evidence, and marketing-claim review based on the published formula, product information, and scientific literature.

Quick answer

Medicube PDRN Pink Peptide Serum is worth considering as a hydrating, glow-focused peptide serum, not as a clinic-level regenerative treatment. The best-supported benefits are moisture, temporary plumping, and possible gradual brightening from niacinamide and the overall formula. Evidence that intact salmon-derived PDRN penetrates normal facial skin and drives major collagen repair is not established.

Product snapshot and the two-formula problem

Medicube currently sells two materially different products from the same U.S. product page: Salmon DNA PDRN and Rose PDRN, marketed by Medicube as vegan. They do not share the same ingredient list. Sodium DNA appears earlier in the salmon formula’s published INCI order, which also includes Milt Extract, Salmon Egg Extract, hydrolyzed collagen, and atelocollagen. The Rose version lists Sodium DNA close to the end and removes the named salmon-derived adjuncts.[1]

The “1% PDRN” claim needs context

OLIVE YOUNG Global currently describes the salmon version as containing 1% or 10,000 ppm salmon PDRN.[2] The accessible Medicube U.S. page does not publish an independent content assay, molecular-weight distribution, certificate of analysis, or residual-protein result for the finished serum. A percentage in retailer copy tells you what is marketed. It does not tell you the size of the DNA fragments, their purity, their stability in the bottle, or how much reaches living skin.

The product page appeared inconsistent with its ingredient lists when reviewed

When reviewed on July 4, 2026, Medicube’s U.S. product page stated that the serum was formulated without fragrances, EDTA derivatives, and acrylates. However, the ingredient lists displayed on the same page included Fragrance, Disodium EDTA, and multiple acrylate polymers. This may be a product-page documentation error, but until clarified, consumers should rely on the published INCI lists rather than the broader “formulated without” section.[1]

Transparency warning

A brand can correct a webpage. Until it does, consumers should rely on the current INCI list rather than the “free from” block. The documented inconsistency lowers our transparency score.

What PDRN is and what the research actually proves

PDRN, short for polydeoxyribonucleotide, is a mixture of DNA fragments rather than whole sperm cells. Pharmaceutical and research preparations are commonly obtained from purified salmon or trout sperm DNA. Reviews often describe a broad molecular-weight range of roughly 50 to 1,500 kDa, although the exact distribution depends on source and manufacturing.[4]

Two mechanisms are usually discussed. First, PDRN breakdown products may support signaling linked to the adenosine A2A receptor. Second, nucleotides and nucleosides may contribute to the salvage pathway, which helps stressed tissue reuse building blocks for nucleic-acid synthesis. Preclinical work links these pathways with angiogenesis, fibroblast activity, inflammation control, and tissue repair.[4]

That biology is meaningful, especially in damaged tissue. It is not direct proof that a cosmetic serum visibly rebuilds healthy facial skin.

These studies support the statement that PDRN can be pharmacologically active. They do not support the stronger claim that an over-the-counter serum delivers the same material to the same tissue depth at an effective dose.[5][6][7][8]

Can topical PDRN penetrate intact skin?

The “500 Dalton rule” is an empirical rule of thumb, not a law of physics. It says that passive skin penetration becomes increasingly difficult for molecules above about 500 Da. Classic PDRN fragments are measured in kilodaltons, which places them orders of magnitude above that threshold.[9]

That does not prove zero penetration. Hair follicles, damaged barrier, specialized carriers, very small fragments, and assisted delivery can change transport. It does mean the brand needs finished-product penetration data before anyone can confidently say that large DNA fragments reach the viable epidermis or dermis in meaningful amounts.

The 2022 microneedling study is often used too broadly

A 2022 study evaluated PDRN alone and a PDRN, vitamin C, and niacinamide mixture in cells and a UVB-exposed animal model. In the animal experiment, the researchers used a microneedling therapy system to improve delivery. They reported changes in pigmentation, oxidative-stress markers, collagen, and elasticity-related markers.[10]

This is useful preclinical evidence. It is not a trial of Medicube, not a trial in human faces, not PDRN alone in every comparison, and not evidence for ordinary passive use. The study was funded by I’ll Global Inc., which should be considered alongside its preclinical design and lack of passive human facial testing. The correct conclusion is that benefit was demonstrated under assisted-delivery conditions, not that the Medicube serum has been shown to do the same.

The newer low-molecular-weight plant PDRN study

A newer paper tested a cream containing 1% low-molecular-weight PDRN derived from Paeonia lactiflora. The human clinical portion included only 10 participants and reported a 4.25% increase in periorbital elasticity after four weeks. The material was plant-derived, the formula was product-specific, skin penetration was not directly demonstrated, and company-affiliated researchers were involved. It does not validate salmon-derived PDRN or Medicube’s finished serum.[11]

It is an interesting proof of concept. It does not validate salmon PDRN, Medicube’s molecular-weight profile, or the exact formula in this bottle.

PDRN is scientifically interesting. The missing step is proof that this finished serum delivers an active form through intact skin.

Real ingredient, incomplete cosmetic evidence.

Salmon PDRN formula analysis

Unless otherwise stated, the detailed ingredient analysis below refers to the Salmon DNA PDRN version. The Rose PDRN version has a materially different ingredient list and should not be treated as the same formula.

The salmon formula is not a one-ingredient product. It is a large hydrating and texture-focused formula with niacinamide, multiple humectants, an emollient, film-forming polymers, five peptides, adenosine, hyaluronic acid, CoQ10, fragrance, botanicals, and several salmon-derived ingredients.[1]

1. Hydration and texture base

Glycerin, Glycereth-26, butylene glycol, dipropylene glycol, polyglycerin-3, and sodium hyaluronate form a credible humectant system. Based on its humectant system, this part of the formula has the strongest potential to increase surface hydration, provide temporary plumping, and create a smoother-looking appearance. A human study of 0.1% hyaluronic-acid formulations reported improvements in hydration and elasticity, with molecular weight affecting wrinkle outcomes. Medicube does not disclose its hyaluronic-acid concentration or molecular weight.[12]

Isopropyl myristate is high in the list and provides slip and emollience. It is often labelled “comedogenic” from older models, but a rabbit-ear test does not reliably predict whether a finished human facial formula will clog an individual person. A more accurate warning is that the emollient system may not suit every very clog-prone user.

2. Niacinamide is one of the stronger ingredients here

Niacinamide appears high in the salmon INCI. A 12-week double-blind split-face study using 5% niacinamide in 50 women reported improvements in fine lines, texture, hyperpigmented spots, red blotchiness, and yellowing. The study was industry affiliated, and Medicube does not reveal whether its concentration matches the tested 5%.[13]

Even at a lower concentration, niacinamide can contribute to a well-designed hydrating serum. It is a more evidence-based explanation for gradual tone improvement than the product’s PDRN headline.

3. Sodium DNA and the salmon-derived extras

Sodium DNA is the ingredient Medicube presents as PDRN. The INCI name alone does not prove that the material matches pharmaceutical PDRN in molecular-weight distribution, purity, residual protein, stability, or biological activity.

Milt Extract and Salmon Egg Extract add to the salmon identity of the formula. A small 66-woman study reported cosmetic improvements from formulas containing 1% or 5% salmon egg extract, but that work was product-specific and does not establish benefit at Medicube’s undisclosed concentration.[14]

Hydrolyzed collagen and atelocollagen are more defensible as surface-conditioning or film-forming ingredients than as proof of new dermal collagen. The product does not publish evidence that these ingredients cross intact skin at a concentration that remodels the dermis.

Palmitoyl pentapeptide-4 has the best direct cosmetic evidence in this group. A split-face trial in 93 women reported improvement in photoaged facial skin with 3 ppm palmitoyl-KTTKS. The evidence base remains small and industry-linked, and the result cannot be transferred to Medicube without knowing the dose and formulation.[15]

A small 24-person randomized pilot trial evaluated topical acetyl hexapeptide-8 in people receiving botulinum-toxin treatment for blepharospasm. It did not establish cosmetic wrinkle improvement, and results from this medical setting cannot be directly applied to this serum.[16] A separate small split-face serum study did not demonstrate a clear added wrinkle benefit from Argireline.[17]

A final peer-reviewed safety assessment concluded that acetyl hexapeptide-8 amide was safe in current cosmetic practices up to 0.005%, while the available data were insufficient to determine safety above that concentration. That is a safety conclusion, not proof that the peptide reduces facial wrinkles.[26]

Copper tripeptide-1 can be retained in human skin in laboratory models, but laboratory penetration is not the same as visible clinical rejuvenation. A small post-laser study of a copper peptide complex did not show a clear objective advantage over control.[18][19]

5. Adenosine, CoQ10, botanicals, and vitamin B12

Adenosine has modest human anti-wrinkle evidence from finished-product studies. One randomized blinded evaluation reported improvements in wrinkle-related surface measurements, although the tested products contained more than adenosine alone.[20]

Ubiquinone, or CoQ10, has some topical penetration and antioxidant evidence. Medicube’s concentration is not disclosed, so it should be treated as a supporting antioxidant rather than a headline active.[21]

Neem, turmeric, holy basil, and Corallina extracts add antioxidant and botanical marketing value, but there is little direct human evidence that each one changes wrinkles or pigmentation at the levels used here. Fragrance is the clearest established allergy concern in this formula; individual tolerance to the numerous additional extracts is less predictable.

Cyanocobalamin, or vitamin B12, plausibly supplies the pink colour, as the brand states. It is not a well-established topical anti-aging active in this formula.

6. Fragrance and the technical ingredients

Fragrance has no necessary therapeutic role and is a recognized source of cosmetic allergic contact dermatitis. It does not mean everyone will react, but it weakens the claim that this is an obvious choice for sensitive skin.[22]

The remaining polymers, emulsifiers, chelators, pH adjusters, and preservative boosters support stability, spreadability, and product structure. They are not anti-aging actives. Their presence is normal. The issue is that the brand’s “formulated without” copy claims some of them are absent.

Safety, irritation, and who should skip it

The serum is marketed as a cosmetic. In the United States, ordinary cosmetics generally do not receive FDA premarket approval, apart from applicable colour-additive requirements. “Dermatologist-tested” and “low-irritating” are brand claims unless the test protocol, sample size, comparator, and adverse-event data are published.[23]

Fish allergy

Purified DNA itself is not the same as a fish protein allergen. The finished product has not been specifically studied in people with fish allergy. Because the salmon formula contains named salmon-derived extracts, people with a severe fish allergy should ask an allergist before use or choose another product. This is a precaution, not evidence that the serum commonly causes fish-allergy reactions.

Pregnancy and breastfeeding

No pregnancy- or breastfeeding-specific data were identified for this serum or for topical PDRN cosmetics. The current evidence does not establish that the product is either contraindicated or specifically proven safe during pregnancy. Anyone seeking the most conservative routine should discuss it with their clinician or use a simpler product with better-established pregnancy information.

Do not use it as a microneedling or injection serum

A consumer cosmetic is not a sterile procedural product. FDA guidance notes risks from microneedling devices and does not clear them for use with arbitrary cosmetics, drugs, or blood products. A 2021 FDA warning letter involving PDRN-containing products concerned unauthorized microinfusion devices and drug or device claims, not ordinary topical use.[24][25]

Important

Do not inject this serum, use it in a mesotherapy device, or apply it during home microneedling. Normal intact-skin use and barrier-disrupting delivery are different safety situations.

How to use it and what it can be combined with

  1. Apply after cleansing and toner. Use a thin layer on dry or slightly damp skin.
  2. Follow with moisturizer. The serum hydrates, but dry skin may still need a cream to reduce water loss.
  3. Use sunscreen in the morning. Sunscreen is more important for preventing uneven tone and photoaging than any PDRN serum.
  4. Start once daily if reactive. Increase only when your skin remains comfortable.
  5. Patch test for several days. This cannot rule out every reaction, but it may catch obvious fragrance or botanical intolerance.

Retinoids, vitamin C, acids, and benzoyl peroxide

No specific incompatibility between this serum and common retinoids, vitamin C products, exfoliating acids, or benzoyl peroxide has been demonstrated. However, the exact combinations have not been clinically tested, and layering several active products can increase irritation depending on their formulations and your skin. Introduce one new product at a time, avoid escalating several actives in the same week, and reduce frequency if your skin becomes tight or inflamed.

What should come first?

Use thinner water-based products before thicker creams and oils. Medicube recommends morning and evening use, followed by moisturizer and daytime sunscreen. That is a reasonable order, but twice-daily use is not necessary if once daily gives the same comfort and cosmetic result.[1]

Final verdict

Buy it for hydration, glow, and a layered peptide formula. Do not buy it because you expect salmon DNA to reproduce injected PDRN. The product has enough useful ingredients to function as a well-constructed cosmetic serum. Its most dramatic claims are ahead of the finished-product evidence, and the product page would benefit from clearer, internally consistent documentation.

Frequently asked questions

Is PDRN really salmon sperm?

Research and pharmaceutical PDRN is commonly purified from salmon or trout sperm DNA. It is a mixture of DNA fragments, not whole sperm cells. Medicube’s salmon formula also lists Milt Extract and Salmon Egg Extract.

Does topical PDRN actually penetrate skin?

Meaningful penetration from this serum has not been demonstrated. Classic PDRN is very large, so passive movement through intact stratum corneum is a major unanswered question.

Will the serum stimulate collagen?

Some ingredients have laboratory or human evidence related to collagen and fine lines, especially palmitoyl pentapeptide-4 and niacinamide. The exact formula has not been shown to produce major dermal collagen remodelling.

Is it good for acne-prone skin?

It may work for some, but it is not an acne treatment. Very clog-prone users should patch test because the formula contains isopropyl myristate and a substantial emollient system.

Is it suitable for sensitive skin?

The brand says it is low-irritating, but both current formulas list fragrance. Fragrance-allergic or highly reactive users have better fragrance-free options.

Can someone with fish allergy use it?

The finished product has not been specifically studied in people with fish allergy. Because the salmon formula contains named salmon-derived extracts, people with a severe fish allergy should ask an allergist before use or choose another product.

Can I use it during pregnancy?

No direct pregnancy dataset exists for this serum or topical PDRN cosmetics. It is not known to be automatically contraindicated, but a clinician can advise based on individual circumstances.

Can I use it with retinol or vitamin C?

They can generally be used in the same routine, but compatibility depends on the exact products and individual tolerance. Introduce them gradually and reduce frequency if irritation develops.

Can I use it after microneedling?

Do not use this consumer serum as a sterile post-procedure, injection, or microneedling product. Follow aftercare from a qualified clinician.

How fast should it work?

Hydration and glow may appear quickly. Tone and fine-line changes need weeks of consistent use. The brand’s two-week statement is not a substitute for a published independent trial.

Is the Rose PDRN version the same?

No. It has a different INCI, lists Sodium DNA much later, and omits the named salmon extracts. Medicube markets it as vegan.

Is Medicube PDRN serum worth the hype?

Partly. It is a respectable hydrating peptide serum. The salmon DNA story is more compelling than the evidence for passive topical regeneration.
